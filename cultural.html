Cultural & Diversity Contexts

First Nations Families, Immigrant and Refugee Families

Understanding the Context

Cultural and diversity context in the field of early childhood education are concerned with the representativeness of various cultural identities, languages, beliefs and practices in the family that contribute to the experiences of children. This applies to Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander families in Australia and immigrant and refugee families that contribute to different cultural perspectives and experiences. To ensure equitable and inclusive early childhood education, it is important to understand and appreciate this diversity.

In terms of sociology, the ecological systems theory developed by Bronfenbrenner predicts that the development of children depends on the interactions with the surrounding cultural and social environment. Bronfenbrenner (1979) points out that cultural values and the community situations within the community make up the macrosystem which influences the identity of the children and their beliefs as well as their learning experiences. In the case of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander children, a connection to land, culture and community is a key factor of development and wellbeing (Department of Education, 2022).

Also, there is the notion of social determinants of health, which highlights that cultural inclusion, service access and social equity all have a critical bearing on health and development outcomes (Marmot, 2010). The immigrant and refugee families might experience difficulties in terms of language barriers, culture adaptation, and previous traumas, and this may influence children in taking part in the early learning settings.

Cultural diversity is on the rise in modern Australian society, with more and more culturally and linguistically diverse (CALD) families. Simultaneously, national reconciliation and recognition of the Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples are highly valued. Early childhood educators should therefore take culturally responsive practices which recognise and appreciate diversity but deal with inequities.

Impact on Children and Families

Cultural and diversity contexts impresses a significant influence on the identity of children, their sense of belonging, and learning. The cultural background of children should be recognised and respected, and therefore the child would develop a positive self-esteem and positive social contacts. On the other hand, cultural exclusion or discrimination can have a detrimental impact on the emotional wellbeing of the children and their involvement in the learning process (Department of Education, 2022).

Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander children may find identity formation and wellbeing to be affected by being disconnected from culture and community. It is shown that inclusive and culturally safe settings yield better development and access to education (AIHW, 2023). In the case of immigrant and refugee children, the process of becoming accustomed to a new culture can be associated with the learning of a new language, the accommodation to new social norms, and the struggle with former experiences of displacement or traumatic experiences.

Families of various cultural backgrounds might also encounter obstacles in the access of early childhood services, such as the inability to meet language challenges and unfamiliarity with the education system, and they may experience marginalisation. These issues may inhibit family involvement and roles in child education.

Early childhood services must therefore acknowledge the inclusion and participation strengths and knowledge offered by different types of families but also curb impediments to inclusion and participation. Positive results for all children require support of cultural identity and inclusivity.

Social Policy and Australian Responses

Australia has come up with various policies and frameworks to embrace cultural diversity and inclusion in early childhood education. Included within the Early Years Learning Framework provided by the Department of Education, Australia, is the emphasis on respect towards diversity and cultural competence (Department of Education, 2022).

The National Quality Framework of ACECQA demands that educators establish inclusive settings that promote cultural diversity and individual child identity. Also, the Australian Institute of Health and Welfare offers the data illustrating the differences in outcomes among Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander children, which supports the necessity to offer specific support (AIHW, 2023).

The practices of government like Closing the Gap are aimed at reducing inequalities faced by the Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islanders. Equally, multicultural policies endorse the inclusion of the immigrant and refugee families in Australian society. Although these policies form a powerful basis, constant effort must be made to achieve equity in accessing services and practices that are culturally responsive in all early childhood settings.

Strategies for Practice

Early childhood educators play a vital role in promoting cultural inclusion and supporting diverse families. The following evidence-based strategies can be implemented:

  • Culturally responsive pedagogy
    Teachers are urged to integrate the cultures of the children in the curriculum, such as language, traditions and family practices.
  • Building cultural competence
    Teachers are urged to integrate the cultures of the children in the curriculum, such as language, traditions and family practices.
  • Supporting home languages
    Promotion of the use of home languages makes them stronger in identity, communication and family ties.
  • Creating inclusive environments
    Learning spaces should reflect diversity through books, resources, and displays that represent different cultures.
  • Engaging with families and communities
    Working with families and community members will make the cultural knowledge appreciated and spread to practice.

The strategies are consistent with findings of studies that underscore the significance of culturally inclusive practices in enhancing the well-being and learning of children (Marmot, 2010).

Community and Professional Partnerships

In helping the culturally diverse families, collaboration with community organisations is required. Key organisations include the following:

  • SNAICC – supports Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander children and families (SNAICC, n.d.)
  • Multicultural Australia – provides services for culturally diverse communities (Multicultural Australia, 2021)
  • Settlement Services International – supports refugees and migrants
  • Raising Children Network – offers culturally inclusive parenting resources
  • Australian Red Cross – supports refugees and families in crisis (Australian Red Cross, 2022)

The measures that early childhood services have to work with such organisations are referrals and cultural programmes as well as community engagement programmes. Such collaborations assist in guaranteeing that families and children can get culturally appropriate assistance.
