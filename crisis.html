Crisis and Emergency Contexts

Natural Disasters, Family Violence, and Displacement

Understanding the Context

Crisis and emergency situations are those circumstances that interfere with the safety, stability and well-being of children and families. These are natural catastrophes (bushfires and floods), family abuse, and displacement following an emergency or unsafe situations. This kind of event is becoming common in Australia, and on its part it has notable consequences on the development and wellbeing of young children.

In a sociological approach, the ecological systems theory of Bronfenbrenner demonstrates how a child can be impacted by the suddenness of disrupting the environment in which he/she lives to impact several systems of development. Bronfenbrenner (1979) emphasises that family (microsystem) or community (exosystem) crises may also result in instability, stress, and diminished access to the necessary supports. Also, the idea of social determinants of health reminds us that environmental or housing stability and exposure to violence are major determinants of children's health and developmental outcomes (Marmot, 2010).

Australia has experienced bushfires, floods, the COVID-19 pandemic, and so on, which has demonstrated the vulnerability of children and families in times of crisis. The Australian Institute of Health and Welfare notes that family violence and natural disasters are a few of the major contributors to child protection issues and family breakdown (AIHW, 2023). Emergency displacement may also interfere with the routine, relationships, and access to early education of children.

These situations are dynamic and demanding, and early childhood educators must be responsive and adaptive to such situations with a high level of sensitivity and a high level of awareness of the needs of children knowingly in those periods of crisis.

Impact on Children and Families

The development and wellbeing of children may be impacted both in the short run and long run due to crisis situations. Trauma, anxiety and emotional distress may occur as a result of exposure to natural disasters or family violence. Such occurrences may cause children to develop fear, confusion, sleep disturbances, and behaviour changes (Masten & Narayan, 2012).

Prone to disruption of routines and environments, this may cause children to feel unsafe and unstable, critical components of healthy development. To illustrate this, being displaced by the disaster could lead to loss of home, loss of community, and a break in early learning experiences. The disruptions have the potential to affect cognitive development, social relationships, and emotional control.

Another risk of family violence is that the children can be brought directly or indirectly into unhealthy conditions. Studies have shown that the development of the brain, attachment, and the long-term mental health outcomes may be influenced by exposure to violence (World Health Organization, 2021).

Families in a state of crisis are often more stressed, have less money to spend and have few support services. Parents and carers might not be able to offer continuous care, which further affects the wellbeing of children. These aspects indicate the role of early childhood services as a source of stability, support, and recovery-centred services.

Social Policy and Australian Responses

Australia has also come up with policies and emergency response programmes to assist the children and families in case of an emergency. The Department of Education Australia underlines the significance of continuity in early childhood education in emergencies, as it is important so that children can continue to have access to supportive and safe learning environments (Department of Education, 2022).

The Australian Institute of Health and Welfare makes available statistics about family violence, child protection, and disaster impact to inform policy and service delivery (AIHW, 2023). There are national frameworks on disaster resilience and family safety that are intended to minimise risks and aid in its recovery.

In disaster management, organisations like the Australian Red Cross are instrumental in cases of disaster recovery and response in order to offer emergency aid and community services (Australian Red Cross, 2022). The policies related to family violence include prevention, early intervention, and assistance to the affected families.

Although these responses are important, studies have indicated that more united and child-based solutions should be adopted in order to make sure that the early childhood services are well equipped to react in the event of crisis (Masten & Narayan, 2012).

Strategies for Practice

The following evidence-based strategies can be implemented by early childhood educators when working with families and children in the cases of crisis and emergency situations:

  • Providing safe and secure environments
    Physical and emotional safety aids in making children feel safe and cared about in periods of uncertainty.
  • Implementing trauma-informed practices
    Teachers ought to be aware of the indicators of trauma and react in a manner that is understanding, uniform, and gentle (Masten & Narayan, 2012).
  • Maintaining routines and structure
    The routine activities aid in bringing normalcy and stability to those children who have experienced crisis.
  • Supporting emotional expression and coping
    Play, storytelling and discussion are encouraged to help children get through emotional recovery.
  • Collaborating with families and support services
    Collaboration and referral to the relevant services with families will provide comprehensive support and recovery.

The strategies uphold resilience and enable children to overcome negative experiences, which is in line with research on child development and trauma.

Community and Professional Partnerships

Collaboration with community organisations is essential in responding to crisis situations. Key organisations include:

  • Australian Red Cross – provides emergency relief and recovery support
  • State Emergency Service – supports disaster response and community safety (NSW State Emergency Service, n.d.)
  • Lifeline Australia – offers crisis counselling and emotional support
  • Beyond Blue – provides mental health resources
  • 1800RESPECT – supports individuals experiencing family violence

Early childhood services may partner with these organisations by way of referrals, emergency planning, and family support programmes. Such unions will enhance resilience within the communities and make sure that children and families get assistance in a timely manner.
